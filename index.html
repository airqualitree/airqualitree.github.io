Air. Water. Land.

Where you live and who you are defines your relationship with the environment. This project seeks to explore, and to understand, the environmental injustices faced by everyday people in Los Angeles County.

What is Environmental Justice?

What happens to our trash after it leaves our houses? Who decides where power plants are built? And most importantly, who bears the burden for the polluted air, water, and soil created by our overconsumption? The 20th century environmental movement was integral for establishing regulatory organizations that provide for our clean air and water, but it wasn’t until the late 1970s that environmental inequities were brought to the mainstream. From Black-led protests in Houston against the building of a landfill close to public schools in 1979 to the present day, activists demonstrated that marginalized communities often suffered the worst pollution, whether it be via the construction of factories or landfills or less funding for infrastructure. And Los Angeles is no exception — a battery factory released lead into the atmosphere, resulting in dangerously high lead concentrations in the soil around southeast Los Angeles. Although the factory was closed in 2015, the lead-contaminated soil has not yet been cleaned.

What is this website?

Airqualitree aims to use data to study the intersection of air quality, water quality, green space, and environmental justice in Los Angeles. We strive to understand a community’s environmental health and to connect the science to socioeconomic and demographic data in order to reveal the human experience behind pollution. For this project, we analyzed air and water quality data from the CalEnviroScreen 3.0 report, a screening tool created by the CalEPA and California State Department in 2017 to summarize the state of California’s environment. This report comprehensively examines pollution indices, geography, income levels, demographics, and other pertinent variables across the state. Additionally, for green space, we analyzed Los Angeles tree canopy data from Jarlath O’Neil-Dunne, a scientist at the University of Vermont’s Spatial Analysis Lab. Our project looked for correlations between a community’s demographic variables and its environmental burden to determine whether communities of color, as well as those with lower income levels, experience higher PM2.5, water pollutant concentrations, and/or less green space.

Our Guiding Research Questions

1

What connections or relationships can we draw between air/water quality and socioeconomic data?

2

What are the short and long term health consequences of poor air and water quality on individuals?

3

What is the relationship between air quality, water quality, and access to greenspace?
